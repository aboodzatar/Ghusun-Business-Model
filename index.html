Market Opportunity

Why Jordan,
Why Now

Local

~35,000 Greenhouses in Jordan

Jordan's Jordan Valley and Highlands regions have historically counted roughly 23,800 and 11,000 greenhouses respectively (FAO figures) — the country's small-farm structure means a large share of these are individually owned and operated, exactly the retrofit customer GHUSUN is built for. This figure is dated and likely understates today's count, but it establishes a real, sizable addressable base within one country.

Regional

Water Scarcity Driving Demand

Agriculture consumes about 65% of total water use across the MENA region, making water-efficient automation a structural need rather than a nice-to-have. The global precision agriculture market was valued at roughly $9.3B in 2024 and is projected to more than double by 2033 — with the Middle East specifically flagged as an early-stage, fast-growing adopter driven by water scarcity and food security pressure.

Where GHUSUN
Fits

Smart Green (Jordan)

A Jordan-based agritech startup founded in 2020, best known for Fertigo, a smart irrigation and fertigation system addressing water scarcity through IoT and cloud-based control.

GHUSUN differs by covering the full greenhouse stack — sensing, ventilation, lighting, and irrigation together, with an LLM-based agentic decision layer and plant-level computer vision — rather than irrigation alone.

Pure Harvest Smart Farms (UAE)

Adjacent — Different Segment

A capital-intensive controlled-environment agriculture company that builds and operates its own high-tech greenhouses at scale, having raised over $180M to date.

GHUSUN targets the opposite end of the market: existing smallholder farmers retrofitting greenhouses they already own, at a fraction of the capital cost, rather than new-build industrial infrastructure.

We have not identified a direct competitor in Jordan or the wider MENA region combining multi-actuator automation, agentic AI decision-making, and computer-vision plant health analysis in a single retrofit-friendly system.

Hardware Strategy

Actuator Approach —
Retrofit or Full Kit?

Option A — Retrofit Mode

Lower Barrier to Entry

GHUSUN integrates with the farmer's existing actuators — pumps, fans, vents — using smart relay switches. If a farmer has a manual button for ventilation, a relay module sits inline and GHUSUN "presses" it digitally. No need to replace working hardware.

Relay module interfaces with existing wiring
Servo/mechanical actuator for physical switches if needed
Zero infrastructure destruction
Installation time: 2–4 hours
Ideal for farmers with existing greenhouses
Recommended as the default entry point. Lowers switching cost dramatically and builds trust before the farmer commits to new hardware.

Option B — Full Kit

Turnkey Solution

GHUSUN provides the complete hardware stack — sensors, controller, pumps, fan, LED grow lights — all pre-wired, tested, and ready to deploy. Ideal for new greenhouses or farmers with no existing automation.

ESP32-S3 controller unit (GHUSUN-branded)
DHT11, soil moisture, LDR sensor array
Irrigation pump(s) + relay board
Ventilation fan + LED grow light strip
Weatherproof enclosure + power supply
Installation + commissioning by GHUSUN team
Higher upfront revenue per customer. Sold as an add-on or bundled with Pro/Enterprise tiers. Commands premium pricing due to turnkey convenience.

Revenue Model

Three Streams,
One Product

Stream 01

Hardware Sales

One-time revenue from selling the GHUSUN controller unit, sensor kit, and optional full actuator kit. Margin is built in above raw component cost. Retrofit kits are lower cost but higher volume. Full kits command premium pricing and justify higher setup fees.

Stream 02

Subscription (SaaS)

Monthly recurring revenue from software access, AI decision quota, dashboard features, and support tiers. This is the most predictable and scalable revenue stream. As the user base grows, per-user backend costs decrease while revenue grows linearly.

Stream 03

AI Overage & Add-ons

Pay-per-use revenue when farmers exceed their monthly AI decision quota. Additional plant profiles, advanced analytics exports, and custom reporting for cooperatives or government partners also fall here. Predictable usage generates predictable revenue.

Stream 04 — Future

Data & Partnerships

As GHUSUN accumulates aggregated, anonymized farm data across Jordan, this dataset becomes valuable to seed companies, the Ministry of Agriculture, NGOs like FAO, and agricultural research institutions. B2G (business-to-government) partnerships are a realistic long-term revenue channel.

AI Roadmap

From LLM to
Local Intelligence

A phased approach to reducing API dependency and building a competitive moat in agricultural AI.

Phase 1 — Now (Current)

OpenAI API (GPT-4o-mini) powers all AI decisions. Agentic reasoning with confidence scores and severity levels. Cost is per-API-call — manageable at small scale but not sustainable at thousands of farms. This is the right approach for a student project and early deployment.

Gemini runs as a separate CV pipeline (Pro Farm & Cooperative tiers) for pest and disease image detection — decoupled from the GPT-4o-mini decision engine, so the two models can be swapped or upgraded independently.

Phase 2 — In Progress

Rather than replacing the LLM, GHUSUN is being extended with a lightweight prediction layer that forecasts near-term environmental trends — soil moisture and temperature trajectories, for example. These forecasts, combined with live sensor readings and computer-vision plant health analysis, feed richer context into the LLM, so its decisions become more proactive, explainable, and context-aware — without losing the transparency and dynamic reasoning of an LLM-based agent. This hybrid design (predictive layer + agentic LLM) is more robust than either approach alone, and reduces reliance on the LLM for pattern recognition it isn't naturally suited for — which also lowers per-decision API cost over time.

Phase 3 — 3+ Years

LLaMA-based model deployed locally on-device or on a local server at each farm. No internet required for AI decisions. Full data privacy. No ongoing API cost. This is the competitive moat — a locally-running, Jordan-specific agricultural AI that no enterprise system offers.

Every automated decision passes through a safety and semantic validation layer with built-in cooldowns before any actuator command is issued — no AI output reaches hardware unchecked.

A product priced too low signals low quality — especially for something managing a farmer's livelihood. The guarantee itself justifies higher pricing: the farmer pays more, but carries zero risk. That is a premium offer, and should be priced as one.
